0Introduction

Where do our crops come from?

Of the roughly 350,000 known plant species, fewer than 900 are cultivated for food worldwide. This explorer turns Prof. Rubén Milla's Crop Origins & Phylo Food database into an interactive atlas — letting you trace each crop back to the wild progenitor's biogeographic realm, plot its climate envelope, and follow its taxonomic and evolutionary lineage.

The interface is split in two: a Global Explorer covering all 867 crops, and a separate Antiquity Atlas chapter restricted to the 211 species for which a published domestication date exists. The two sets of filters are independent — moving the antiquity sliders does not constrain the rest of the explorer, and vice versa.

Scroll to navigate, or use the rail on the left to jump between sections. Press / at any time to focus search, Esc to close any open panel.

1Origin Atlas

Where wild progenitors are most abundant

Each crop is plotted at the centroid of the ecoregion (Olson et al. 2001) where its wild progenitor has the most GBIF occurrence records. Marker colour encodes biogeographic realm; the soft gradient communicates that the location is an indicator of origin region, not the precise domestication site.

AAntiquity Atlas — separate chapter

The 211 dated crops

Of the 867 species in the database, only 211 carry a published estimate for minimum_time_domestication. From here on, every visualization is restricted to that subset and uses an independent set of filters (the orange block in the sidebar) — so anything you set here will not affect the Global Explorer above.

Why 211? The original Milla 2020 paper notes that domestication antiquity is documented for a minority of crops. Records range in quality from a single grey-literature reference to multi-source archaeobotanical and genomic syntheses, and all values are minimum estimates. Crops without a date are simply omitted from this chapter — they are still present everywhere else in the explorer.
